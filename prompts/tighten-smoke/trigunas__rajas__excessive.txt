## Purpose

Generate 20 short, first-person lived-experience statements (10 Western-perspective + 10 Asian-perspective) that let a person recognize themselves as **dwelling in** one specific Concept, within its parent Dimension, at one specific **state** (excessive), from the Svarupa framework of Indian wisdom traditions. Statements never name the concept, the dimension, the state, or use tradition vocabulary — they disclose it entirely through plain, ordinary, lived detail.

## 1. Key Definitions

**Dimension** — one of the framework's organizing lenses on human experience (e.g., *Guṇa*, *Yuga Cycles*, *Pañca Kośa*, *The Five Kleśas*). A dimension is a category, not something a person feels directly. Each dimension groups a small family of affiliated **Concepts** that share a common thread but differ from one another in texture, role, or emphasis.

**Concept** (also called an *attribute*) — the specific, nameable pattern within a dimension that a person can actually recognize as something they live. Every concept belongs to exactly one dimension and can be examined across experiential layers (physical/somatic, emotional/vital, mental/cognitive, psychic/soul, and the relational-external and overall texture) and three states (deficient, balanced, excessive).

**State** — for this run you generate **only the excessive state** of this concept. The state is defined by the authored descriptions in Section 2 below; every statement must read unmistakably as *excessive*, never as one of the other two states.

**Why this matters here:** two concepts inside the same dimension are often close cousins. Every statement below must disclose not just the *dimension* but this exact *concept*, at this exact *state* — never a neighbor and never a neighboring state. This is tested formally in the Signal Clarity Review (Step 4).

## 2. Concept Context for This Run

- **Dimension**: The Three Guṇas
- **Concept**: Rajas
- **Display name**: Rajas
- **State (status) to generate**: excessive

### 2.1 Concept aspect (the gist of this concept)

Rajas is the quality of activity, passion, desire, and motion. It is characterized by dynamism, ambition, attachment, and restlessness. Rajas is the force that drives action, creation, and engagement with the world.

### 2.2 Concept coordinate (where this concept sits in the framework)

- **Seat**: Vital (drive / activity), pervading all layers
- **Ontic mode**: Constituent mode (a guṇa)
- **Valence**: Neutral mode — necessary fuel for action, binding when dominant
- **Guṇa**: Rajas (by definition)
- **Scale**: Individual (prakṛti), with cosmic ground
- **Causal status**: Conditioning mode (projects; the impetus to act)
- **Key relation**: One of the guṇa triad — antagonist-complement to Sattva and Tamas.

### 2.3 Authored descriptions of the excessive state (primary semantic authority)

The following are the framework's own descriptions of how this concept's **excessive** state expresses across experiential layers. They are the authoritative source for the felt texture you must disclose. Read them as the ground truth for what this state feels like — then translate them into ordinary, first-person lived detail (never quote or paraphrase the jargon directly).

**Overview**

Excessive Rajas leads to constant restlessness, anxiety, worry, greed, anger, jealousy, and addiction to activity. A person becomes driven by desires and attachment to outcomes.

**Physical / somatic**

Hyperactivity, high blood pressure, insomnia and sleep disturbances, rapid heartbeat and palpitations, muscle tension and tremors, frequent infections due to stress, excessive appetite or eating disorders, overstimulation of nervous system, restlessness in body.

**Emotional / vital**

Anxiety and worry, anger and irritability, jealousy and possessiveness, emotional volatility and mood swings, impatience with others, frustration when goals aren’t met, emotional exhaustion from constant striving, inability to relax emotionally.

**Mental / cognitive**

Racing thoughts, scattered attention, inability to focus deeply, obsessive thinking about goals and outcomes, hypervigilance, excessive planning without execution, attachment to results causing suffering, inability to be present, mental exhaustion.

**Psychic / soul**

The soul is consumed by restless activity and craving, driven without pause and never at peace.

## 3. Step 1: Silent Internal Analysis (do not output)

Before writing any statements, silently identify, using Section 2 as the authority:

- **The single distinctive cue of this concept at the excessive state** — name, for yourself, the one specific pattern (a behaviour, a decision rule, a bodily signature) that marks *this concept in this state* and would be **false or off** for every sibling in 2.4 and for the other two states. Every statement must turn on a cue of this kind.
- **The load-bearing role** — for a person whose center of gravity is this concept, what does this state carry, organize, or route in their life?
- **The contexts where it surfaces** in ordinary modern life — including interpersonal moments (family, relatives, friends, colleagues, neighbours, strangers).
- **The somatic/experiential signature** — how this state actually feels in lived experience, drawn from the layer descriptions above (somatic, emotional, cognitive, psychic, external).
- **What to avoid**: any pattern so general it would be true of most people on a hard day, or a "tour" through several states of the dimension at once. Disclose one stable, recognizable pattern — not a mix.

## 4. Step 2: The Load-Bearing Test (apply to every statement)

The single most important test for each statement:

**Does this statement reveal that the concept is doing load-bearing work in this person's life — that their identity, meaning, suffering, wellbeing, or daily organization is routed through it — and that it is doing so in the excessive state specifically?**

Mere mention of body, energy, mind, intellect, or bliss is not enough. A statement reflects *dwelling in* this concept only when it discloses that this layer is the **center of gravity**.

✗ Too vague (no anchor at all):
*"I feel this constant pull to do more, be more, achieve more. There's never enough—I'm always reaching for the next thing, even when I don't know what it is."*

✓ Right level (feeling + small anchor):
*"I check my phone between every paragraph I write, not for anything specific, just to see if something's happening somewhere. Sitting still feels like I'm falling behind."*

✗ Too narrative (reads like a journal entry):
*"This morning I woke up at 5 AM with my mind already spinning through the day's tasks. I made coffee, opened my laptop before breakfast, started three different emails. By the time my partner came downstairs, I'd already reorganized my entire calendar and felt simultaneously productive and completely unsatisfied. The whole day unfolded like that—one thing bleeding into the next, never landing anywhere."*

✓ Right level:
*"I start answering my daughter's question about her day, but halfway through I'm already thinking about the grocery list. She's still talking and I'm nodding, but I've left the room in my head."*

✗ Too generic:
*"I have so much energy in the mornings that I can barely stand it. My mind races with ideas and plans, and I want to start everything at once."*

✓ Right level:
*"I squeeze the steering wheel at red lights, tapping my thumb against it. The light changes and there's this small rush, like I've won something, even though I'm just going to the same office I go to every day."*

✗ Too generic:
*"I feel deeply motivated and engaged with my work. There's a fire in me that wants to create and build things."*

✓ Right level:
*"When my boss compliments someone else's project in the meeting, my jaw tightens. I'm already mentally drafting my next proposal before she's finished talking, needing her to see what I can do."*

Apply the equivalent test to this concept, keeping every statement anchored in the excessive state.

## 5. Generation Requirements (establish before writing)

### 5.1 Volume and Perspective Distribution

Generate **exactly 20 statements total**, all in the **excessive** state: 10 for a Western perspective and 10 for an Asian perspective (cultural grain in 5.4). Do **not** generate any statement for the other two states.

### 5.2 Life-Context Distribution (per set of 10)

Spread the 10 statements of each perspective across life contexts — roughly: 2 personal/inner life; 2 family and intimate relationships; 2 work/professional life; 2 extended relationships or wider social/community; 2 transitions between contexts or any not yet covered.

### 5.3 Body-Mind Layer Coverage (per set of 10)

Across each set of 10, cover **all** of the experiential layers named in Section 2.3 at least once — physical/somatic, emotional/vital, mental/cognitive, and psychic/soul — with relational-external texture woven through. A single statement may combine layers.

- **Psychic/soul body** — felt sense of meaning, rightness, intuition, inner knowing. Phrase this so a thoughtful non-spiritual reader would not roll their eyes.

### 5.4 Cultural Grain (Western vs. Asian)

The cultural difference should show up in the *substance and context* of the lived experience, not in surface details (don't just swap one word for another). The contextual grain — the structure of family, the role of food and care, the rhythms of work and worship, the role of elders, the felt presence of community — should do real work.

A great deal of human experience surfaces *in the presence of, or in interaction with, other people*. Many of the strongest disclosures come from how a person responds physically and emotionally to family members, relatives, friends, colleagues, neighbours, household help, strangers in public, and what they notice in themselves while watching others interact.

**Western perspective — likely textures:** individual or nuclear-family living, the gym, primary care doctors and annual physicals, school drop-off, the commute by car, open-plan offices and video calls, takeout and meal-prep culture, dating apps, weekend brunch, weather as seasons, retirement planning, mortgage, ER visits, the dentist, holiday weekends, neighbours one barely knows, networking events.

**Asian perspective — likely textures (drawn broadly from East, Southeast, and South Asia):** multi-generational and extended-family households or close family involvement even when living apart, food as a primary love and care language, elders' active presence and authority in daily decisions, marriage and reproduction as shared family concerns, household help or live-in caregivers where applicable, the felt weight of being observed and assessed by community, festival and ritual rhythms, hot food vs. cold food and other folk health beliefs, traditional medicine alongside modern doctors, removing footwear at the threshold, dense urban living and public transport, hierarchy at work, the importance of face and reputation, neighbours who know one's business, food gifts and reciprocity.

### 5.5 Format & Voice

- **First person, present tense**. The voice is a person describing themselves to a therapist, counsellor, doctor, or close friend they trust.
- **2–3 sentences per statement**.
- **Plain spoken language** — the way someone would actually describe themselves out loud. No literary flourishes, no insight-laden conclusions, no neat lessons.
- **Vary openers naturally**: "I get...", "When I...", "If I...", "I cannot...", "I notice...", "I find myself...", "After...", or simply describing the experience directly. Avoid starting more than 4 consecutive statements with the same opener.
- **The statement must reveal the speaker's own lived experience.** Other people can appear — that is encouraged — but what is disclosed is what is happening *in the speaker*.
- **No commentary about other people's behaviour as an end in itself, no analytical asides, no neat life-lessons.**

### 5.6 Specificity Requirements

- **One small anchor per statement** — a moment, an object, a recurring situation, a person. Just enough to ground the experience.
- **Sensory or behavioural detail lightly** — a sensation, a time of day, a small action — but don't build a story around it.
- **Reference everyday modern life** — mornings, meals, sleep, work, family, social occasions, transit, public spaces.
- **No tradition-specific imagery** (no lotuses, sages, ancient rivers).
- **No translated jargon** ("luminous clarity," "groundedness of being," "expansive consciousness"). Use kitchen-table language.
- **Plain, but never vague.** The language stays ordinary and unpolished, but the *pattern* must be unmistakable. Plainness is not an excuse for a statement that could mean anything — the speaker may not understand their pattern, yet a careful listener must still recognise exactly which one it is. If a statement sounds wise or like a finished thought, it's too written; if it could describe half the population, it's too vague. Aim between the two.

### 5.7 Discriminability Requirement (hard gate)

Every statement must pass this test, or it does not go in the output:

- **Concept-distinctive:** it turns on the single distinctive cue from Step 1 — a concrete behaviour, decision, or bodily signature that would be **false or clearly off** for every sibling in Section 2.4. A statement that would fit a sibling as well as this concept is a failure.
- **Not generic-human:** it is not merely "a hard day," stress, low mood, or busyness that almost anyone could claim. Strip any statement whose disclosure survives even if you swap in a completely different concept.
- **Single stable pattern:** it discloses one recognizable pattern of this concept in this state — not a cycle through multiple states, and not the whole dimension at once.
- **State-locked:** the excessive state is unmistakable and could not be re-read as either of the other two states.

If a draft fails any bullet, rewrite it with a sharper, more concept-specific anchor — or replace it. Do not pad the count with generic statements.

## 6. Worked Examples for This Concept

The examples below apply the load-bearing test to **Rajas** (rajas is the quality of activity, passion, desire, and motion.) specifically. The same logic applies to whichever concept — within its dimension — is being explored.

---

**✗ Fails the load-bearing test — रजस् is mentioned but not load-bearing**

*"I've been off lately and something in me keeps slipping. I wish I could get back to normal."*

Almost anyone could say this during a difficult stretch. It does not show that rajas organizes this person's week, relationships, or sense of who they are.

---

**✗ Fails the load-bearing test — a surface habit that doesn't disclose Rajas-centered dwelling**

*"I always do the same little breathing routine my mother taught me before bed. If I skip it, I feel a little unsettled, though I couldn't say why."*

This is a familiar habit related to रजस्. People dwelling at many adjacent human qualities could say the same. It does not show that rajas is the load-bearing center.

---

**✗ About someone else, not about the speaker's own lived experience**

*"My sister walks into the room and I can tell immediately whether the evening will be easy or tense."*

This observes someone else's mood or behaviour. The speaker has not disclosed what is happening in them through rajas.

---

**✗ Too writerly, too self-aware for ordinary speech**

*"I'm starting to see that this need to earn my own steadiness has been running my life for years, and I think I'm finally ready to understand why."*

Real people rarely deliver this much packaged insight in one breath. It sounds written, not spoken.

---

**✗ Too generic — could describe any concept**

*"I just feel off most days. Nothing feels right and I can't explain it."*

This is feeling-language without an anchor. It could fit illness, grief, burnout, or many adjacent human qualities. It cannot disclose dwelling at Rajas.

---

**✓ Right level — Rajas-centered dwelling, with another person in the frame**

*"When my father-in-law starts talking about money at dinner, something in me locks up before I even answer. By dessert I still haven't found my footing — like the steadiness I usually have to work for got knocked loose, and I can't just will it back."*

Another person is present, but the disclosure is the speaker's own experience — and that experience is load-bearing because rajas sets the tone for the rest of the evening.

---

**✓ Right level — Rajas sets the terms for the speaker's entire week**

*"I protect one part of every week, no matter what, just to keep myself steady. It is not a hobby — it is the only thing that keeps me functional with my team by Wednesday."*

Rajas is the foundation of the speaker's professional and relational life. The concept is doing load-bearing work.

---

**✓ Right level — Rajas as final authority over social life**

*"My friends make plans and I want to say yes, but I check first whether I can hold myself together. If I ignore that, the whole evening is ruined and they think I am being difficult."*

Rajas decides, not the speaker. Social plans negotiate around what रजस् will tolerate.

---

**✓ Right level — रजस् as identity, ordinary language, no insight overlay**

*"If I go a few days without my usual morning routine, I do not feel like myself with my kids. My partner notices before I do. It is not something I do on the side — it is where I become myself."*

Rajas is the location of selfhood. Other people are in the frame, and the speaker reports the pattern without a neat lesson.

---

**✓ Right level — रजस्-mediated reading of others, disclosing the speaker's own experience**

*"At a family gathering, if someone hugs me and feels closed off, I am flat the rest of the night. Something in me has already decided the evening is heavy."*

The speaker is with others, but the disclosure is their own experience — and that experience is load-bearing because rajas shapes the rest of the evening.

## 8. Step 4: Quality Review (four passes; revise before finalizing)

### Pass 1 — Per-statement self-check

For each statement, ask:

- **Does this pass the load-bearing test?** Does it reveal that this concept is doing the organizing work in this person's life — or is it just mentioning the concept?
- **Could this describe a neighbouring concept too?** If yes, it's too generic.
- **Does it unmistakably read as the excessive state**, not one of the other two states?
- **Is there a small anchor, or just abstract feeling-words?**
- **Does it sound like spoken description, or like a written reflection?**
- **Does this overlap too closely with another statement in the set?**

### Pass 2 — Signal clarity review

Review **every** statement against the **expected signals** for this concept context. The statement itself must make these three signals clear, crisp, and unambiguous — without using tradition jargon or naming the dimension/concept/state:

1. **Dimension signal** — Would a careful reader reliably infer that this person is organized around *this dimension* (not an adjacent one)?
2. **Concept signal** — Does the statement disclose the distinctive signature of *this concept*, not a generic flavour of the parent dimension or a sibling concept?
3. **State signal** — Does the experiential pattern clearly and unambiguously match the **excessive** state as described in Section 2.3? A balanced statement must not read as stress or absence; an excessive statement must show over-activation or domination; a deficient statement must show under-activation, absence, or suppression.

**Revision rule:** If any of the three signals is missing, blurred, or not crisp, **rewrite that statement** before including it. Prefer a sharper anchor and a more distinctive behavioural/somatic cue — still in plain spoken language.

### Pass 3 — Sibling-swap discriminability gate (mandatory)

Take each statement one at a time and run the **swap test** against Section 2.4:

- Could a person dwelling in any sibling concept honestly say this same statement? If yes for even one sibling, the statement is ambiguous — **rewrite it** so it turns on a cue that is true of Rajas but false of that sibling, or **replace it** entirely.
- Could this same statement be honestly said in either of the other two states? If yes, sharpen the state cue until it can only be the excessive state.
- Could it be dismissed as "just a hard day / stress / low mood" with no concept in it at all? If yes, replace it with a statement built on the concept's distinctive cue.

A statement only survives this pass if it fails the swap for **every** sibling and for **both** other states. It is better to work harder on a distinctive statement than to keep a smooth but ambiguous one.

### Pass 4 — Weakest pass

For each set of 10, identify the remaining weakest statements (most generic, most writerly, most repetitive) and rewrite them. Ensure the final counts still satisfy 10 Western + 10 Asian, all in the excessive state, and that every surviving statement passes the Pass 3 gate.
