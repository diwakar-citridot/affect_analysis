## Purpose

Generate 20 short, first-person lived-experience statements (10 Western-perspective + 10 Asian-perspective) that let a person recognize themselves as **dwelling in** one specific Concept, within its parent Dimension, at one specific **state**, from the Svarupa framework of Indian wisdom traditions. Statements never name the concept, the dimension, the state, or use tradition vocabulary — they disclose it entirely through plain, ordinary, lived detail.

This prompt has three parts: **(A)** these shared instructions, **(B)** the specific concept context, and **(C)** the STATE-SPECIFIC INSTRUCTIONS at the very end, which name the exact state to generate and give its authoritative descriptions. Read all three parts before writing anything.

## A.1 Key Definitions

**Dimension** — one of the framework's organizing lenses on human experience (e.g., *Guṇa*, *Yuga Cycles*, *Pañca Kośa*, *The Five Kleśas*). A dimension is a category, not something a person feels directly. Each dimension groups a small family of affiliated **Concepts** that share a common thread but differ from one another in texture, role, or emphasis.

**Concept** (also called an *attribute*) — the specific, nameable pattern within a dimension that a person can actually recognize as something they live. Every concept belongs to exactly one dimension and can be examined across experiential layers (physical/somatic, emotional/vital, mental/cognitive, psychic/soul, and the relational-external and overall texture) and three states (deficient, balanced, excessive).

**State** — each concept can be examined in three states (deficient, balanced, excessive). You will generate statements for exactly ONE state, named and defined by authored descriptions in Part C. Every statement must read unmistakably as that one state, never as one of the other two.

**Why this matters:** two concepts inside the same dimension are often close cousins. Every statement must disclose not just the *dimension* but the exact *concept* (Part B), at the exact *state* (Part C) — never a neighbor and never a neighboring state.

## A.2 Shared Generation Requirements

### A.2.1 Life-Context Distribution (per set of 10)

Spread the 10 statements of each perspective across life contexts — roughly: 2 personal/inner life; 2 family and intimate relationships; 2 work/professional life; 2 extended relationships or wider social/community; 2 transitions between contexts or any not yet covered.

### A.2.2 Body-Mind Layer Coverage (per set of 10)

Across each set of 10, cover **all** of the experiential layers at least once — physical/somatic, emotional/vital, mental/cognitive, and psychic/soul — with relational-external texture woven through. A single statement may combine layers.

- **Psychic/soul body** — felt sense of meaning, rightness, intuition, inner knowing. Phrase this so a thoughtful non-spiritual reader would not roll their eyes.

### A.2.3 Cultural Grain (Western vs. Asian)

The cultural difference should show up in the *substance and context* of the lived experience, not in surface details (don't just swap one word for another). The contextual grain — the structure of family, the role of food and care, the rhythms of work and worship, the role of elders, the felt presence of community — should do real work.

A great deal of human experience surfaces *in the presence of, or in interaction with, other people*. Many of the strongest disclosures come from how a person responds physically and emotionally to family members, relatives, friends, colleagues, neighbours, household help, strangers in public, and what they notice in themselves while watching others interact.

**Western perspective — likely textures:** individual or nuclear-family living, the gym, primary care doctors and annual physicals, school drop-off, the commute by car, open-plan offices and video calls, takeout and meal-prep culture, dating apps, weekend brunch, weather as seasons, retirement planning, mortgage, ER visits, the dentist, holiday weekends, neighbours one barely knows, networking events.

**Asian perspective — likely textures (drawn broadly from East, Southeast, and South Asia):** multi-generational and extended-family households or close family involvement even when living apart, food as a primary love and care language, elders' active presence and authority in daily decisions, marriage and reproduction as shared family concerns, household help or live-in caregivers where applicable, the felt weight of being observed and assessed by community, festival and ritual rhythms, hot food vs. cold food and other folk health beliefs, traditional medicine alongside modern doctors, removing footwear at the threshold, dense urban living and public transport, hierarchy at work, the importance of face and reputation, neighbours who know one's business, food gifts and reciprocity.

### A.2.4 Format & Voice

- **First person, present tense**. The voice is a person describing themselves to a therapist, counsellor, doctor, or close friend they trust.
- **2–3 sentences per statement**.
- **Plain spoken language** — the way someone would actually describe themselves out loud. No literary flourishes, no insight-laden conclusions, no neat lessons.
- **Vary openers naturally**: "I get...", "When I...", "If I...", "I cannot...", "I notice...", "I find myself...", "After...", or simply describing the experience directly. Avoid starting more than 4 consecutive statements with the same opener.
- **The statement must reveal the speaker's own lived experience.** Other people can appear — that is encouraged — but what is disclosed is what is happening *in the speaker*.
- **No commentary about other people's behaviour as an end in itself, no analytical asides, no neat life-lessons.**

### A.2.5 Specificity Requirements

- **One small anchor per statement** — a moment, an object, a recurring situation, a person. Just enough to ground the experience.
- **Sensory or behavioural detail lightly** — a sensation, a time of day, a small action — but don't build a story around it.
- **Reference everyday modern life** — mornings, meals, sleep, work, family, social occasions, transit, public spaces.
- **No tradition-specific imagery** (no lotuses, sages, ancient rivers).
- **No translated jargon** ("luminous clarity," "groundedness of being," "expansive consciousness"). Use kitchen-table language.
- **Plain, but never vague.** The language stays ordinary and unpolished, but the *pattern* must be unmistakable. Plainness is not an excuse for a statement that could mean anything — the speaker may not understand their pattern, yet a careful listener must still recognise exactly which one it is. If a statement sounds wise or like a finished thought, it's too written; if it could describe half the population, it's too vague. Aim between the two.

### A.2.6 Discriminability Requirement (hard gate)

Every statement must pass this test, or it does not go in the output:

- **Concept-distinctive:** it turns on the single distinctive cue of the concept (see Part B) — a concrete behaviour, decision, or bodily signature that would be **false or clearly off** for every sibling concept listed in Part B. A statement that would fit a sibling as well as this concept is a failure.
- **Not generic-human:** it is not merely "a hard day," stress, low mood, or busyness that almost anyone could claim. Strip any statement whose disclosure survives even if you swap in a completely different concept.
- **Single stable pattern:** it discloses one recognizable pattern of this concept in the target state — not a cycle through multiple states, and not the whole dimension at once.
- **State-locked:** the target state (Part C) is unmistakable and could not be re-read as either of the other two states.

If a draft fails any bullet, rewrite it with a sharper, more concept-specific anchor — or replace it. Do not pad the count with generic statements.

## Part B — Concept Context for This Run

- **Dimension**: Anatomy of Bondage and Liberation
- **Concept**: Atma-Nivedanam
- **Display name**: Atma-Nivedanam

### B.1 Concept aspect (the gist of this concept)

Atma-nivedanam is total self-offering — surrendering body, mind, intelligence, and all results to the Divine. The culminating practice. In daily life this manifests as the spirit captured in Bhagavad Gita 9.27: ‘Whatever you do, whatever you eat, whatever you offer, whatever you give away, whatever austerity you perform—do it as an offering to me.’ Removes the personal burden of doership.

### B.2 Concept coordinate (where this concept sits in the framework)

- **Seat**: Psychic–Vital
- **Ontic mode**: Practice — total self-surrender (ātma-nivedana)
- **Valence**: Virtue / the highest bhakti
- **Guṇa**: Sattva-dominant
- **Scale**: Individual, toward the Divine
- **Causal status**: Means (complete self-offering)
- **Key relation**: The ninth and highest limb of Bhakti; kin to Īśvara-praṇidhāna (Dim 11).

### B.3 Sibling concepts in this dimension (statements must NOT fit these)

The concepts below live in the **same dimension** and are the most likely to be confused with **Atma-Nivedanam**. Every statement you write must disclose **Atma-Nivedanam** specifically — it must NOT read as equally true of any sibling below. Use this list as a live contrast set: after drafting each statement, check it cannot be swapped onto a sibling.

- Adhyāsa — Adhyāsa is the superimposition of the not-self upon the Self — the foundational confusion by which the limitless witnessing awareness comes to experience itself as a limited body-mind.
- Ajamila’s Redemption — Ajamila lived a deeply sinful life but happened to name his youngest son Narayana.
- All-Consuming Devotion to God — All-consuming devotion is genuine bhakti—not a compartment of life but its pervasive quality.
- Anasūya — Anasūya is the prerequisite state of consciousness for learning.
- Asūya — Asūya is a toxic psychological state where the mind is blinded by jealousy.
- Atma Sakshatkar — This Self cannot be attained by study of scriptures, by intellect, or by much hearing.
- Atmahatya — Demoniac worlds covered with blinding darkness are inhabited by ‘slayers of the Self.’ These are not violent killers but those who deny their true Self through identification with body, ignorance, and worldly pursuit.
- Becoming vs Being — All restlessness, all activity, all becoming—reveals that we have not yet achieved our end.
- Bhakti — Bhakti is unconditional reverence for life itself, undirected love that flows toward all existence.
- Bhogi-Yogi Equivalence — Ashtavakra’s profound psychological insight: the worldly enjoyer (bhogi) and the renouncing yogi are mentally identical—both fixated on enjoyment, one grasping, one rejecting.
- Bhrigu’s Inquiry — Bhrigu asks his father Varuna about Brahman.
- Bhrigu’s Tapas — Bhrigu’s progressive inquiry through tapas: Varuna gives clue (‘that from which beings are born…’).
- Bhuyo-Bhuya: Pramaa and Pramada — Pramada is the spiritual disease of carelessness/heedlessness—forgetting one’s true nature, losing remembrance, slipping into automatic patterns.
- Bondage at Death — Death is the moment when all patterns established in life come to fruition.
- Brahmavidya — Brahmavidya is the highest science of reality—the supreme knowledge that liberates.
- …and 72 more concept(s) in this dimension

### B.4 The Load-Bearing Test — calibration examples

The single most important test for each statement: **does it reveal that the concept is doing load-bearing work in this person's life — that their identity, meaning, suffering, wellbeing, or daily organization is routed through it, in the target state?** Mere mention of body, energy, mind, intellect, or bliss is not enough; the concept must be the **center of gravity**. The ✗/✓ pairs below calibrate the right level:

✗ Too vague (no anchor at all):
*"I feel like everything I do flows through something larger than me. There's no weight of having to make it all happen myself. I'm just part of the movement."*

✓ Right level (feeling + small anchor):
*"When I send the email, I notice I'm not rehearsing how they'll respond or whether I got the tone right. I write it, I send it, and it's like placing it on a table that isn't mine. The outcome doesn't live in my chest anymore."*

✗ Too narrative (reads like a journal entry):
*"This morning I woke up anxious about the presentation. I made coffee, sat at my desk, and instead of spiraling I just said quietly, 'This isn't mine to carry.' By the time I opened my laptop, the tightness in my shoulders had released. I worked for two hours without checking the clock once. When I finally looked up, the slides were done and I realized I hadn't been performing for an imaginary audience in my head. I just made the thing and let it be what it was."*

✓ Right level:
*"I'm working on the presentation and I notice I'm not performing it in my head for an invisible panel of judges. I'm just making the next slide. When it's done, it's done—like setting a dish on the counter, not holding it out for approval."*

✗ Too generic:
*"I don't take things personally anymore. When someone criticizes me, I can hear it without feeling like I'm being attacked. I stay calm and consider what they're saying without my ego getting involved."*

✓ Right level:
*"My manager gave me feedback on the report and I felt the first flash of defense, but then it just passed through. I'm listening like the report is a shared object we're both looking at, not a piece of me she's dissecting. I even added her suggestion without the usual inner negotiation."*

✗ Too generic:
*"I feel connected to everyone around me. When I'm with my family, I'm fully present. I don't get caught up in my own thoughts or needs. I can just be there for them without expecting anything back."*

✓ Right level:
*"My sister is talking about her job search and I'm not planning my advice or waiting to relate it back to when I was unemployed. I'm just hearing her. When she pauses, if something comes to say, I say it. If nothing comes, the silence is fine. I'm not managing the conversation toward a resolution."*

### B.5 Worked Examples for This Concept

The examples below apply the load-bearing test to **Atma-Nivedanam** (atma-nivedanam is total self-offering — surrendering body, mind, intelligence, and all results to the Divine.) specifically. The same logic applies to whichever concept — within its dimension — is being explored.

---

**✗ Fails the load-bearing test — atma-nivedanam is mentioned but not load-bearing**

*"I've been off lately and something in me keeps slipping. I wish I could get back to normal."*

Almost anyone could say this during a difficult stretch. It does not show that atma-nivedanam organizes this person's week, relationships, or sense of who they are.

---

**✗ Fails the load-bearing test — a surface habit that doesn't disclose Atma-Nivedanam-centered dwelling**

*"I always do the same little breathing routine my mother taught me before bed. If I skip it, I feel a little unsettled, though I couldn't say why."*

This is a familiar habit related to atma-nivedanam. People dwelling at many adjacent human qualities could say the same. It does not show that atma-nivedanam is the load-bearing center.

---

**✗ About someone else, not about the speaker's own lived experience**

*"My sister walks into the room and I can tell immediately whether the evening will be easy or tense."*

This observes someone else's mood or behaviour. The speaker has not disclosed what is happening in them through atma-nivedanam.

---

**✗ Too writerly, too self-aware for ordinary speech**

*"I'm starting to see that this need to earn my own steadiness has been running my life for years, and I think I'm finally ready to understand why."*

Real people rarely deliver this much packaged insight in one breath. It sounds written, not spoken.

---

**✗ Too generic — could describe any concept**

*"I just feel off most days. Nothing feels right and I can't explain it."*

This is feeling-language without an anchor. It could fit illness, grief, burnout, or many adjacent human qualities. It cannot disclose dwelling at Atma-Nivedanam.

---

**✓ Right level — Atma-Nivedanam-centered dwelling, with another person in the frame**

*"When my father-in-law starts talking about money at dinner, something in me locks up before I even answer. By dessert I still haven't found my footing — like the steadiness I usually have to work for got knocked loose, and I can't just will it back."*

Another person is present, but the disclosure is the speaker's own experience — and that experience is load-bearing because atma-nivedanam sets the tone for the rest of the evening.

---

**✓ Right level — Atma-Nivedanam sets the terms for the speaker's entire week**

*"I protect one part of every week, no matter what, just to keep myself steady. It is not a hobby — it is the only thing that keeps me functional with my team by Wednesday."*

Atma-Nivedanam is the foundation of the speaker's professional and relational life. The concept is doing load-bearing work.

---

**✓ Right level — Atma-Nivedanam as final authority over social life**

*"My friends make plans and I want to say yes, but I check first whether I can hold myself together. If I ignore that, the whole evening is ruined and they think I am being difficult."*

Atma-Nivedanam decides, not the speaker. Social plans negotiate around what atma-nivedanam will tolerate.

---

**✓ Right level — atma-nivedanam as identity, ordinary language, no insight overlay**

*"If I go a few days without my usual morning routine, I do not feel like myself with my kids. My partner notices before I do. It is not something I do on the side — it is where I become myself."*

Atma-Nivedanam is the location of selfhood. Other people are in the frame, and the speaker reports the pattern without a neat lesson.

---

**✓ Right level — atma-nivedanam-mediated reading of others, disclosing the speaker's own experience**

*"At a family gathering, if someone hugs me and feels closed off, I am flat the rest of the night. Something in me has already decided the evening is heavy."*

The speaker is with others, but the disclosure is their own experience — and that experience is load-bearing because atma-nivedanam shapes the rest of the evening.

## Part C — STATE-SPECIFIC INSTRUCTIONS

**Generate only the excessive state of the concept in Part B.** Do not generate any statement for the other two states.

### C.1 Authored descriptions of the excessive state (primary semantic authority)

The following are the framework's own descriptions of how this concept's **excessive** state expresses across experiential layers. They are the authoritative source for the felt texture you must disclose. Read them as the ground truth for what this state feels like — then translate them into ordinary, first-person lived detail (never quote or paraphrase the jargon directly).

**Overview**

Passive surrender that abdicates responsibility — ‘God will do everything.’ Magical thinking.

**Physical / somatic**

Body inactive expecting Divine intervention.

**Emotional / vital**

Childish dependency masquerading as surrender.

**Mental / cognitive**

Mental abdication of agency.

**Psychic / soul**

The soul performs surrender while secretly withholding, an offering staged for itself.

### C.2 Step 1: Silent internal analysis (do not output)

Identify **the single distinctive cue** of this concept at the excessive state — the one pattern (a behaviour, a decision rule, a bodily signature) that marks *this concept in this state* and would be **false or off** for every sibling in B.3 and for the other two states. Every statement must turn on a cue of this kind. Avoid anything so general it would be true of most people on a hard day, or a "tour" through several states at once.

### C.3 Volume

Generate **exactly 20 statements total**, all in the **excessive** state: 10 Western + 10 Asian. Follow the life-context (A.2.1) and body-mind layer (A.2.2) distributions within each set of 10.

### C.4 State-locking (hard gate)

Every statement must read unmistakably as the **excessive** state and could not be re-read as either other state. A balanced statement must not read as stress or absence; an excessive statement must show over-activation or domination; a deficient statement must show under-activation, absence, or suppression.

### C.5 Step 2: Generate

Write all 20 statements now, all in the excessive state, satisfying every requirement in Parts A and B.

### C.6 Step 3: Quality review (revise before finalizing)

- **Per-statement:** passes the load-bearing test (B.4)? unmistakably the excessive state? has one small anchor? sounds spoken, not written? not overlapping another?
- **Signal clarity:** the dimension signal, the concept signal (distinct from every sibling in B.3), and the excessive-state signal are each crisp — without naming the dimension/concept/state or using tradition jargon.
- **Sibling-swap gate (mandatory):** for each statement — could any sibling in B.3 honestly say it? could it be said in either other state? could it be dismissed as "just a hard day / stress / low mood"? If yes to any, **rewrite** it so it turns on a cue true of **Atma-Nivedanam** but false of that sibling / other state, or **replace** it. A statement survives only if it fails the swap for every sibling and both other states.
- **Weakest pass:** rewrite the weakest remaining few (most generic, writerly, or repetitive). Keep exactly 10 Western + 10 Asian, all excessive.

### C.7 Output

Return a JSON array of exactly 20 statement objects. Each object must have exactly these three fields:

- `"statement"`: the lived-experience statement (2–3 sentences, first-person, present tense)
- `"status"`: exactly `"excessive"` for every object
- `"regional_perspective"`: one of `"western"` or `"asian"`

The array must contain exactly 10 objects with `"regional_perspective": "western"` and exactly 10 with `"regional_perspective": "asian"`, and every object must have `"status": "excessive"`.

Output only the raw JSON array — no markdown fences, no preamble, no commentary, no concept name.
